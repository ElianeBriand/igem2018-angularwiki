Project Design

Project genesis

Our team GO Paris Saclay started its journey focusing on two main projects : one named ‘Cytodestiny’ aimed to develop and optimize, in a yeast model, functionalized nanobodies targeting α-synuclein aggregates responsible of Parkinson's disease; another one, the ‘Heterogenious Cleaning Factory’ designed to develop a bacterial platform capable of cleaning up hospital wastewater ,contaminated with therapeutic anticancer agents. After a constructive peer-review session with our advisors and PIs, the ‘Heterogenious Cleaning Factory’ was finally chosen according to the following motivations :

Anti-cancer drugs are chemotherapeutic agents, designed to kill or reduce proliferating cells. Most of these drugs are not completely metabolized by the patients, therefore are released in the environment in urine.

Unfortunately, environmental risk assessment is available for only a select few anti-cancer drugs, derived mainly from predicted data and excluding information on their metabolites and transformation products (TPs). It is known that the most commonly used drugs such as methotrexate (MTX) or 5-fluorouracil exhibit genotoxic effects due to their mechanism of action.

The few available studies investigating these drugs show their presence in post-treatment wastewater meaning that the water treatment processes are not able to degrade them entirely. Here we describe how our project was designed with the purpose of degrading/transforming anti-cancer drugs.

As proof of concept, we decided to focus on methotrexate (MTX), one of the most used anti-cancer drugs used, found in high concentration in untreated hospital wastewater. As a folate analog, MTX acts as a chemotherapeutic drug by inhibiting dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR), an enzyme that reduces folic acid to tetrahydrofolic acid. Therefore, MTX interferes with DNA metabolism and cell replication. It is used to treat various diseases (e.g. cancer, rheumatoid arthritis, psoriasis ...). Since MTX is highly toxic, its presence in the hospital wastewater is worrying.

Design of the methotrexate transformation pathway

Figure 1 : Overview of the chemical transformation produced by our pathway

For MTX biotransformation inside the cell, we focused our attention on two enzymes that have been described as involved in MTX biotransformation (Figure 1). The most important one is the Pseudomonas carboxypeptidase G2 (CPG2, commercialy marketed as Voraxasetm) which rapidly converts MTX into less toxic metabolites glutamate and DAMPA . We also tested the interest of co-expressing a bifunctional dihydrofolate synthase and folylpoly-γ-glutamate synthetase (FolC) that could potentially enhance MTX catabolism by coupling MTX to polyglutamate. By increasing its accumulation within the cell, FolC could allow higher effective MTX concentration and reaction rate. We hypothesized that in presence of FolC, the MTX is rapidly linked to polyglutamates, a reaction that could increase its metabolism by limiting its export and so that increasing the activity of CPG2.

For the MethotrExit project, we introduced in our chassis an expression cassette encoding cpg2 gene under the control of lac promoter coupled or not to folC gene under the control of ara promoter.

Choice of chassis and indicative strains

Many laboratory strains of E. coli are resistant to MTX. This resistance is mainly due to the export of MTX through efflux pumps composed several proteins such as AcrA and TolC . One of the challenges of our project therefore relays on limiting MTX efflux to favor MTX biotransformation and sequestration within the ‘cleaning factory’. Moreover we needed an indicative strain to easily monitor MTX biotransformation.

We decided to take advantage of the availability in our lab of the Keio collection to test the growth of several E. coli K12 mutants (ΔacrA1, ΔacrB, ΔabgT, ΔtolC) in presence of MTX (Figure 2).

Main conclusions: The WT strain may constitute a suitable ‘MTX-resistant chassis’, whereas ΔacrA1 and ΔtolC strains could constitute MTX-sensitive indicative strains as well as chassis accumulating MTX. Since TolC is a general efflux pump component involved in a large panel of compound efflux (even in standard LB growth conditions), we thought it could be interesting to also evaluate its general interest as a chassis sequestrating MTX (and possibly other toxic molecules). Finally, the ΔacrA1 mutant was used as an indicative strain for further bioassays since it presents no growth defect in absence of MTX.

Expression vector design

Figure 3 : The folC-cpg2 encoding plasmid present within the MTX ‘Cleaning Factory’

FolC/folylpolyglutamate: adding a polyglutamate to MTX
FolC is one of the enzymes (ATP-dependent) which can drive the glutamylation reaction. FolC is naturally encoded by E. coli. However to control and overexpress it, we cloned folC under the control of the ara inducible promoter in pSB1C3.

Carboxypeptidase G2: breaking MTX into DAMPA and glutamate
Originating from Pseudomonas strain RS-16, carboxypeptidase is not naturally present in E. coli genome. The cpg2 gene was cloned within pSB1C3 under the control of lac inducible promoter. We constructed two plasmids: one containing only cpg2 expression cassette and the other harboring both cpg2 and folC expression cassettes separated by a transcription terminator (Figure 3).

The translation units in this expression vector were synthetized separately, courtesy of IDT, then assembled using standard biobrick assembly.

The experimental scheme used for this study is summarized in the Figure 4.

Figure 4 : MTX-biotransformation assays

The WT and ΔtolC chassis strains harbouring pSB1C3-cpg2, pSB1C3-folC-cpg2 or control plasmids (pSB1C3-tet or pSB1C3-folC) and were precultured overnight under agitation at 37°C in 4 ml of LB supplemented with 30 µg/ml chloramphenicol. Thereafter, the chassis strains were inoculated at an opacimetry of 0.2 (DO600nm) in LB medium containing and 0.5 mM IPTG and 0.2 % arabinose and either 2% DMSO (‘control condition’) or 514 µM MTX (‘MTX condition’). Five or 24 h after shaking at 37°C, the samples were harvested, centrifuged and the supernatants filtered before being tested on the ΔacrA1 indicative strain or submitted to HPLC analysis.

Main conclusions for the bioassay: The MTX supernatants incubated during 20 h with the chassis strains (WT or ∆tolC) harboring a plasmid encoding cpg2 under the control of lac promoter (pSB1C3-cpg2 or pSB1C3-folC-cpg2) are not toxic anymore against the ∆acrA1 indicative strain (Figure 5). The best growth yields of the indicative strains are observed when the « Cleaning Factory » is the ∆tolC mutant harboring pSB1C3-folC-cpg2.

Figure 5 : HPLC analysis of MTX medium incubated with a control strain or a ‘MTX-Cleaning Factory’
The results correspond to the HPLC analysis of supernatants obtained after 5 h of incubation with WT strains harboring pSB1C3-tet (CLTR) or pSB1C3-folC-cpg2 (‘MTX Cleaning factory’) bacteria as described in Figure 4. The HPLC assay was run with a reverse phase, C18 column. Detection was made using UV spectrophotometry at 303 nm (peak absorption 302-303 nm) with peak-triggered UV-vis spectrum (diode array). The eluting solvent was a gradient of acetonitrile and water (with 0.1% v/v HCOOH). This mode of operation is very similar to the assay and impurities protocols of reference pharmacopeia for MTX.

Main conclusions for the HPLC analysis: MTX is removed from the culture medium in presence of bacteria harboring cpg2 encoding plasmids as early as 5 h post-incubation. Of note, the supernatants incubated only 5h with the chassis were tested but still present some toxicity against the ∆acrA1 indicative strain (data not shown). We hypothesize that waiting longer (20 h) allow the bacteria to remove a toxic compound from the medium.
