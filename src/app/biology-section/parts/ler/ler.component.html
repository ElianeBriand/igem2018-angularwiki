Ler
Ler regulator series ler — BBa_K2688019 to BBa_K2688021 A transcription regulator protein that binds to LEE5 promoter sites, counteracting H-NS silecing and allowing transcription. Several variants are available, with design implications. Registry pages: Ler1 Ler2 Ler3

Executive summary: Ler (LEE-encoded regulator) belongs to the H-NS family of silencers. However, contrary to H-NS, it allows expression of the downstream genes. We have demonstrated that Ler Biobricks can activate LEE5 and that the ‘HeteroGenious’ LEE5-Ler device can generate a heterogeneity of transgene expression within a clonal population. This study opens new insights on the design of ‘noise’ in synthetic biology.

Introduction

Ler (LEE-encoded regulator) belongs to the H-NS family of xenogeneic silencers. This protein is encoded by the LEE pathogenicity island present in some pathogenic A/E (attachment/effacement) Escherichia coli, Citrobacter rodentium and Escherichia albertii . Its main action is to activate the expression of the pathogenicity island by counteracting H-NS silencing. Of note, Ler is neither toxic nor pathogenic.

During this project we cloned three versions of Ler (Figure 1):

Ler1

Ler protein of E. coli 3030-1 (GenBank: EGW78798.1). Leh and collaborators have shown that Ler1 can activate LEE5 promoter in K12 E. coli heterologous context

Ler2

Ler protein of enteropathogenic (EPEC) E. coli E2348/69 (NCBI Reference Sequence: WP_001253284.1).
In its native context (EPEC), Ler2 is required to activate LEE5 expression under appropriate conditions.
Moreover, the Ler2 binding sites on LEE5 region have been characterized by DNA footprinting.

Ler3

Ler2 K34M is a spontaneous mutant of Ler2 that was obtained during cloning.
We wanted to characterize further the impact of the K34M mutation on Ler2 activity.

Cloning of Ler biobricks

The native ler1 gene contains forbidden sites. Therefore, we designed an optimized version of this gene (devoid of forbidden restriction sites). This ler1 version was synthetized downstream a synthetic RBS and cloned within pSB1C3-tetO (BBa_R0040), generating pSB1C3_tetO_RBS_ler1 (BBa_K2688022).

To construct pSB1C3_ RBS_ler_2 (BBa_K2688019), the native ler2 gene with its RBS was amplified by PCR using GO2018-7 (CCGCTTCTAGAGAATAATTTTGTTTAACTTTAAG) and GO2018-8 (CGCAGTACTGCAGCGGCCGCTACTAGTATCATTAAATATTTTTCAGCGGTATTATTTC) primers from a lab collection plasmid of Leh and collaborators . The PCR product was digested and cloned within pSB1C3, generating pSB1C3_ tetO_RBS_ler2 (BBa_K2688023). After sequencing, we obtained a clone harboring plasmids with the expected sequence and another clone that contained a plasmid encoding a mutated version of ler2 gene. Since this was a false-sense mutation affecting a positively charged residue (K34M) we further characterized the impact of this mutation on Ler2 activity. The plasmid encoding this mutant version of ler is pSB1C3_ tetO_RBS_ler3 (BBa_K2688024).

Procedure of analysis of Ler activity on LEE5-gfp expression cassette

To analyse the regulation of LEE5 by ler based-biobricks, we used E. coli K12 MG1655 Z1 attPhi80ΩLEE5-gfp reporter strain harboring single integrated copy of the LEE5-gfp reporter cassette (Bury-Moné’s lab collection, Figure 2). This strain contains a Z1 cassette ensuring the expression of TetR (repressor of tet promoter) . Moreover a LEE5 reporter cassette was inserted at attPhi80 site within this strain using J118 vector as previously described . Chemically competent MG1655 Z1 attPhi80ΩLEE5-gfp bacteria were transformed with pSB1C3-tet-RBS-ler1 (BBa_K2688022), pSB1C3-tet-RBS-ler2 (BBa_K2688023), pSB1C3-tet-RBS-ler3 (BBa_K2688024) or the control plasmid pSB1C3-tet (BBa_R0040) and selected on chloramphenicol (30 µg/ml). The expression of ler genes was induced by adding anhydrotetracyclin (aTc, 1 to 100 ng/ml) to the medium. After 24 h growth at 37°C, bacteria fluorescence was assessed by flow cytometry using a Cytoflex S apparatus (Beckman Coulter). Mg1655 Z1 harboring pZA31-MCS plasmid was used as a negative control of fluorescence.

Results

As illustrated on Figure 3, we observed that MG1655 Z1 attPhi80ΩLEE5-gfp presents a very low basal fluorescence [as compared to the negative control without any reporter gene, ‘CTRL1’], suggesting that LEE5-gfp reporter cassette integrated at attPhi80 site is very well silenced in absence of Ler.

In absence of tet inducer (no aTc), MG1655 Z1 attPhi80ΩLEE5-gfp harboring ler1 or ler3 gene on the multicopy vector pSB1C3 are 18-fold or 1.6-fold more fluorescent than the control strain (with pSB1C3-tet, ‘CTRL2’), respectively.

When increasing Ler1 expression by addition of a range of aTc, we did not observe a huge change on LEE5 activity, suggesting that LEE5-gfp expression is already almost maximal with a basal expression of ler1 from pSB1C3. Interestingly, the increase of Ler2 and Ler3 expressions resulted in an increase of LEE5-gfp expression for the lowest tested concentrations of aTc (1 and 5 ng/ml). This indicates that Ler2 is active, but much less effective than Ler1 in regard to LEE5-gfp activation in E. coli K12. We also confirmed that Ler3 is slightly more active than Ler2 in these conditions.

Finally higher expression of Ler2 and Ler3 (10 to 100 ng/ml of aTc) reduces LEE5 activity, suggesting that the ‘silencer’ properties of Ler2 and its derivate may be revealed in presence of a high amount of proteins. Perhaps that the oligomerization of these proteins on LEE5 could silence the gene. Alternatively high concentrations of Ler2 and its derivate might be toxic for the cell (and so detrimental for LEE5 activity).

We used MG1655 Z1 harboring pZA31-MCS (‘CTRL1’) as a negative control of fluorescence.

The analysis of the distribution of the bacterial population depending on LEE5 activity revealed a great heterogeneity (Figure 4). The percentage of GFP+ cells can vary from 1 to ≈ 90 % depending on the Ler variant used and on its level of expression. Moreover the mean intensity of fluorescent bacteria can vary from on a dynamic range of 1 to 3-log in the same culture experimental condition! Finally we observed for some experimental conditions (e.g. strain harboring pSB1C3-tet-RBS-ler1 cultured with 50 or 100 ng/ml aTc) a bimodal pattern of expression which represents a paroxysm of non-genetic phenotypic heterogeneity. This bimodal pattern of expression is obtained for the highest concentrations of inducer of Ler1, suggesting that Ler1 can also have a dual impact (activation vs. silencing) at high concentration.

Conclusion: Whereas Ler1, Ler2 and Ler3 sequences are very similar (Figure 1), these proteins behave quite differently regarding their effect on attPhi80ΩLEE5-gfp cassette. We have demonstrated that Ler Biobricks can activate LEE5 and that the ‘HeteroGenious’ LEE5-Ler device can generate a heterogeneity of transgene expression within a clonal population. This study opens new insights on the design of ‘noise’ in synthetic biology.

Note: We further aimed to find a constitutive promoter that generates a level of Ler expression suitable to obtain de bimodal pattern of LEE5 activity. We were therefore interested by the Anderson promoter collection. After sequencing the pSB1C3 plasmid harbouring four promoters of this collection we decided to develop an approach to improve one of these Biobricks.
